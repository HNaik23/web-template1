Misconception 1: Mediation and Arbitration Are the Same

Many people believe that mediation and arbitration are interchangeable. While both fall under the umbrella of ADR, they are fundamentally different:
• Mediation is a non-binding process where a neutral third party (the mediator) facilitates discussions between the disputing parties to help them reach a voluntary agreement.
• Arbitration is a binding or non-binding process where a neutral arbitrator hears evidence and renders a decision, which may be legally enforceable.

Misconception 2: Mediation Always Ends in a Settlement

While mediation is designed to encourage settlements, it does not guarantee an agreement. The success of mediation depends on the willingness of the parties to negotiate and compromise. If mediation fails, parties can still pursue arbitration or litigation.

Misconception 3: Arbitration Is Always Faster and Cheaper Than Litigation

Arbitration is often touted as a quicker and less expensive alternative to litigation. However, this is not always the case. Complex arbitration cases can involve lengthy proceedings, expert testimonies, and high arbitrator fees, making it comparable in cost and duration to court litigation.

Misconception 4: Mediators and Arbitrators Make Legal Decisions

A mediator does not have the authority to impose a decision on the parties. Instead, they help facilitate discussions and propose possible solutions. An arbitrator, on the other hand, functions similarly to a judge and renders a decision based on presented evidence, but only if the arbitration is binding.

Misconception 5: Arbitration Favors Businesses Over Individuals

While some critics argue that arbitration clauses in consumer contracts favor businesses, arbitration can be a fair process when structured properly. Many arbitration institutions have rules ensuring fairness, and courts can invalidate arbitration agreements deemed unconscionable.

Misconception 6: Mediation Is Only for Family or Workplace Disputes

Mediation is commonly used in family law and employment disputes, but it is also widely applied in commercial, construction, healthcare, and international disputes. Businesses frequently use mediation to resolve contract disputes and avoid costly litigation.

Misconception 7: Arbitration Awards Are Not Enforceable

Arbitration awards, especially international ones, are enforceable under the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards. Over 170 countries recognize and enforce arbitration decisions, making them a viable solution for cross-border disputes.

Misconception 8: Mediation and Arbitration Lack Legal Oversight

While mediation and arbitration occur outside of court, they are still governed by legal frameworks. Arbitration follows strict procedural rules, and mediated settlements can be legally binding if formalized in a contract.
